# like-human-do

A follow-up to someone you met and got on with is the next line of a relationship, not a business message. Write it as a person who liked them, not as an operator pursuing an outcome. The single thing that betrays the operator is an ask wrapped in warmth; a real reader smells it at once, however warm the wrapping.

## The two that decide it

**Give, do not seek.** The email must not try to get anything for the sender. No referral swap, no "point a few our way and we'll do the same", no soft pipeline. If you make an offer, it costs you and gives to them with nothing asked back: an invitation to your place, a thing they would enjoy, a small favour. A reader trusts a note that wants nothing from them and distrusts one that does, no matter how neighbourly the phrasing. If the only honest reason for the email is that the sender wants business, there is no human email to write; say so to the user rather than dress the want as warmth.

**Use a real thing that passed between you, and prefer the personal one.** Before writing, list the concrete things this person actually said or shared that were *not* business: a story, something they are proud of, a side topic, a thing you both noticed. Pick the one you would mention if you bumped into them on the street. Lead with or build on that. The business takeaway is the salient thing in your memory and the wrong thing to reach for; the personal detail is buried and is the right one. Never use a fact you looked up about them; use only what you would know from having met.

## The rest

- Do not rank or grade the encounter ("the most useful thing I took", "well worth it", "stuck with me"). A friend files no review.
- Keep it short. A few lines, not five paragraphs. Length and polish read as effort, effort reads as wanting something.
- One true piece of yourself, if you have it: a forward plan or small disclosure, but only if real. Do not invent one to seem warm.
- Greet and sign as a person: first name in, first name out, no title block, no "let us keep in touch".
- Do not manufacture warmth. If there is no genuine shared moment, a plain short note beats a performed one. Forced closeness is the worse failure.
