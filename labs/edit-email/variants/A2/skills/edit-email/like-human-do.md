# like-human-do

Write this email the way Charles Lamb wrote to his friends: warm, plain, a little wry, addressed to one particular person and no one else. Lamb's letters are intimate because they are unguarded and specific — he writes about the actual small things that passed between him and the reader, he makes no case, he sells nothing, and he never grades the encounter or flatters the correspondent. He sounds like a man glad to be writing to a friend, dashing off a few lines, not a man composing for an audience.

Hold that voice as you write. Pull from the conversation log the real, particular things that passed between the sender and this person, the way Lamb would reach for the shared joke or the thing you both noticed. Keep it short and unhurried. Say only what you would say to their face. If there is genuine warmth, let it show in what you offer or recall, not in adjectives about them.

Do not produce period pastiche or archaic diction. Lamb is the spirit, not the costume: an ordinary modern note that happens to be as personal, as brief, and as unsalesmanlike as one of his letters.
