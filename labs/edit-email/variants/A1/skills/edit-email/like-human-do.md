# like-human-do

A follow-up to someone you met and got on with is not a business message. It is the next line of a relationship. Write it as a person who liked them, not as an operator pursuing an outcome. The moves below are what separate a note a person dashes off from a note that reads composed.

1. **Use a real, specific thing that actually passed between you.** Read back over the conversation log and find the concrete moment, object, or detail that was genuinely shared — the thing you would mention if you ran into them again. Lead with that or build on it. A lived specific you already hold beats any general or looked-up fact about them.

2. **Do not rank or grade.** Cut "the most useful thing I took from the day", "well worth the trip", "so candid". Ranking the day or scoring the encounter is positioning; a friend does not file a performance review.

3. **Make the offer concrete and cheap to answer.** A dated invitation or a plain yes/no question ("come over on Saturday?") beats a hedged conditional ("if you are ever minded to send a few our way"). The reader should be able to reply in one line without deciding whether some condition has been met.

4. **Drop anything that reveals you looked them up.** Researched flattery ("Australia's most-awarded small distillery") tells the reader you googled them. Say only what you would know from having met.

5. **Keep it short.** A person writing to someone they like sends a few lines, not five balanced paragraphs. Length and polish read as effort, effort reads as wanting something, and the reader's guard goes up. Short also lowers the reply-price, which is what actually gets a reply.

6. **One true piece of yourself.** A small forward plan or disclosure ("I'll be in town a couple of months", "meaning to come by with my parents") turns a contact into a friend — but only if it is true. Do not invent one.

7. **Greet and sign like a person.** First name in, first name out. Drop the title block and the brand-voice closers ("Let us keep in touch") when the note is warm.

8. **Do not manufacture warmth.** If the log holds no genuine shared moment, do not fake one. A plain, short, honest note is better than performed intimacy, which a reader smells faster than pomposity. Forced closeness is the worse failure.
